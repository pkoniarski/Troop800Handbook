1. Welcome

Congratulations — your child is joining Troop 800, Chula Vista! Troop 800 is a coed Scouts BSA troop chartered by Chula Vista Presbyterian Church (CVPC) at 940 Hilltop Drive. Whether your Scout is crossing over from Cub Scouts or joining for the first time, the troop is about to become a second family of sorts: a place to build friendships, grow into leadership, get outside, and learn skills that will serve them for life.

This handbook is meant to demystify the experience for you, the parent or guardian. Read through once, then keep it as a reference. If something here does not match what the troop is actually doing in a given week, defer to the troop — every troop runs a little differently within the same national framework.

Big Idea. Scouts BSA is youth-led. The Scouts run the meetings and plan the program with guidance from trained adult leaders. Your role as a parent is to support from the sideline, not to direct from the field.

2. About Scouts BSA

Scouts BSA is the flagship program of Scouting America (the national organization formerly known as the Boy Scouts of America) for youth ages 11 through 17. Scouts are organized into troops, which are divided into smaller patrols of about 6–8 Scouts. Troops meet weekly and typically camp one weekend a month, plus a week of summer camp each year.

The Aims of Scouting

  • Character development
  • Citizenship training
  • Leadership development
  • Personal fitness

The Methods of Scouting

The program uses eight methods to achieve its aims: the ideals (Scout Oath and Law), the patrol method, the outdoors, advancement, association with adults, personal growth, leadership development, and the uniform.

The Scout Oath

"On my honor I will do my best to do my duty to God and my country and to obey the Scout Law; to help other people at all times; to keep myself physically strong, mentally awake, and morally straight."

The Scout Law

A Scout is Trustworthy, Loyal, Helpful, Friendly, Courteous, Kind, Obedient, Cheerful, Thrifty, Brave, Clean, and Reverent.

4. Your First 30 Days

Here is a checklist to get your Scout fully set up:

  • Complete registration. Fill out the Scouts BSA youth application. National, council, and troop fees are paid at registration.
  • Submit the Annual Health and Medical Record. Parts A and B are required for participation. Part C (with a physician's signature) is required for events longer than 72 hours, including summer camp.
  • Create a my.Scouting account. Parents can create one to access training and sign electronic forms.
  • Get access to Scoutbook. The official tool for tracking advancement, events, and communications. Your troop will send an invite.
  • Take Safeguarding Youth Training (SYT). Free and online (~30 minutes). Required for any adult at overnight events, and strongly recommended for every parent.
  • Buy a Scouts BSA Handbook. Your Scout needs this book. Advancement is tracked and signed off inside it.
  • Get the uniform. See Section 6. Most troops phase this in; do not feel pressure to buy everything at once.
  • Subscribe to troop communications. Ask where the troop calendar lives and how meeting reminders go out.
  • Attend a parent meeting. The committee meeting is usually the best place to meet other parents and learn how things run.

5. Meetings and the Troop Calendar

Weekly Troop Meetings

Troop 800 meets on Tuesdays at 6:30 pm at Chula Vista Presbyterian Church, 940 Hilltop Drive. A typical meeting lasts about 90 minutes: opening ceremony, skills instruction or patrol time, a game or interpatrol activity, and a closing with announcements. Scouts should arrive in uniform, on time, with their handbook.

Patrol Leaders' Council (PLC)

The PLC is a monthly planning meeting of the youth leaders. This is where the actual program is planned — meeting topics, monthly campouts, service projects. Parents do not attend.

Committee Meetings

The troop committee (adults) meets monthly to handle the business side: finances, registration, transportation, adult training, equipment, and advancement records. Every parent is welcome.

Courts of Honor

A Court of Honor is a ceremony — usually two to four per year — where Scouts are formally recognized for ranks, merit badges, and other awards earned. Come; your Scout will want you there.

6. The Uniform

The uniform is one of the eight methods of Scouting. It is a visible reminder of membership and achievement and an equalizer among Scouts from different backgrounds.

Field Uniform ("Class A")

Worn at weekly meetings, travel to and from events, Courts of Honor, and flag ceremonies.

7. The Outdoor Program

The outdoors is where Scouts BSA really happens. Expect about one campout a month plus a week of summer camp. Troops also pursue "high-adventure" trips as Scouts get older.

Monthly Campouts

Most monthly campouts leave Friday evening and return Sunday morning. Scouts camp by patrol, cook their own meals, and run their own program. Adults camp separately and keep a supportive distance.

Summer Camp

A full week at a council-run camp, typically in June or July. This is where a lot of advancement and merit badges happen. Register and pay early.

High Adventure

Older Scouts (usually 14+) can attend BSA's high-adventure bases: Philmont Scout Ranch (backpacking, NM), Northern Tier (canoeing, MN/Canada), Florida Sea Base (sailing/SCUBA, FL), and Summit Bechtel Reserve (WV). These require year(s) of advance planning, fundraising, and physical preparation.

Standard Personal Gear

  • Sleeping bag rated for the coldest weather you expect
  • Sleeping pad
  • Backpack (start with a school-sized day pack; upgrade to a full backpacking pack before Philmont)
  • Mess kit, water bottle, flashlight or headlamp
  • Rain gear, sturdy shoes or boots, layers (cotton kills — favor wool and synthetics)
  • Scout Handbook and a pen
Before the first campout. Your troop will provide a packing list. Pack it WITH your Scout, not FOR them. Part of the lesson is learning to be responsible for their own gear.

8. Advancement and Recognition

Advancement in Scouts BSA is the Scout's responsibility, not the parent's and not the troop's. Your job is to encourage, not to push.

The Seven Ranks

RankFocus
ScoutJoining rank. Basic knowledge of the Oath, Law, and how the troop works.
TenderfootBasic outdoor skills, fitness, Scout spirit.
Second ClassMore outdoor skills, navigation, first aid, cooking, service.
First ClassCore outdoor proficiency. A "First Class" Scout can camp independently. This is the foundation rank.
StarLeadership role + 6 merit badges (4 Eagle-required). Begins the leadership journey.
LifeMore time in leadership + more merit badges + significant service.
EagleHighest rank. 21 merit badges (13 Eagle-required), significant service project, board of review. Must be completed before the Scout's 18th birthday.

Merit Badges

Merit badges are earned by working with a registered Merit Badge Counselor, using the official pamphlet for the badge. Scouts request a "blue card" from the Scoutmaster, work with the counselor on the requirements, and return the signed blue card. There are over 130 merit badges; many Scouts find a lifelong interest or career through one.

Scoutmaster Conferences and Boards of Review

Before a Scout earns a rank, they sit for a Scoutmaster Conference (a one-on-one conversation with the Scoutmaster) and a Board of Review (a conversation with three to six committee members). These are not tests — they are checkpoints to confirm the Scout has grown and is ready to advance.

Eagle Timeline. The single most common reason Scouts do not earn Eagle is running out of time. Everything must be completed BEFORE the 18th birthday. Most Eagles finish between 16 and 17.5. Plan backwards from your Scout's 18th birthday, not forward from today.

9. Safeguarding Youth and Safety

Scouts BSA has some of the most robust youth-protection policies of any youth organization in the country. Every parent should understand the basics.

Barriers to Abuse — the Key Rules

  • Two-deep leadership. Two registered, SYT-trained adult leaders (one 21+) are required at every Scouting activity.
  • No one-on-one contact. No one-on-one contact between adults and Scouts is allowed, in any form. Parents have full access to any communication between leaders and their Scout.
  • Separate accommodations. Adults and Scouts tent separately; male and female Scouts tent separately; family members may tent with their own Scout only where policy permits.
  • Privacy. Scouts have privacy rights when changing, showering, or using the bathroom.
  • Mandatory reporting. Any suspected abuse — in Scouting or elsewhere — is reported to local authorities and to the Scouts First Helpline at 1-844-SCOUTS1 (1-844-726-8871).

Guide to Safe Scouting

The Guide to Safe Scouting is the BSA's program-wide safety policy document, updated regularly. Your troop follows it. It is free online and worth skimming once.

What You Should Do as a Parent

  • Take Safeguarding Youth Training — even if you never plan to attend an overnight event.
  • Ask the troop how they handle chaperoning, transportation, and communications.
  • Talk with your Scout openly. "Tell me about your weekend" goes a long way.
  • If something feels wrong, trust your gut. Report to the Scoutmaster, the Committee Chair, the council, or the Scouts First Helpline.

10. What It Costs

Scouts BSA is not free, but it is designed to be affordable and most troops help families who need it. Never let cost stop a Scout from participating — ask about scholarships or "campership" funds quietly; almost every troop has them.

Fundraising

Most troops run at least one fundraiser a year — popcorn sales, camp cards, wreaths, or similar. A portion of proceeds often goes into each participating Scout's individual "Scout account," which can be used to offset costs.

Financial Help

If cost is ever a barrier, talk to the Scoutmaster or Committee Chair confidentially. Councils offer camperships (scholarships for summer camp), and troops often have their own assistance funds. The program is committed to keeping Scouting accessible.

11. Your Role as a Parent

Scouting works best when parents stay engaged but step back.

Do

  • Get your Scout to meetings and campouts on time, in uniform, with the gear on the packing list.
  • Encourage advancement progress — but let your Scout drive it.
  • Attend Courts of Honor and celebrate their wins.
  • Take Safeguarding Youth Training.
  • Volunteer where you can.
  • Communicate with the Scoutmaster if your Scout is struggling, sick, injured, or going through something.

Don't

  • Hover over your Scout at meetings. Drop off, say hi, step back.
  • Track advancement for your Scout. It is their responsibility.
  • Jump in when your Scout has a problem with another Scout or a leader. Let them try first.
  • Compare your Scout's pace to another Scout's. The program is individualized on purpose.

Ways to Volunteer

  • Committee member — finances, advancement, activities, membership, fundraising, equipment.
  • Assistant Scoutmaster — active on the program side with the Scouts.
  • Merit Badge Counselor — register to counsel merit badges in your area of expertise.
  • Driver or event chaperone — every campout needs transportation.
  • Behind-the-scenes — sewing patches, coordinating meals, maintaining the troop trailer, running the popcorn sale, taking photos.
Training requirements. Every registered adult leader must complete Safeguarding Youth Training and, for most roles, position-specific training. Most training is free and online at my.scouting.org. California adult volunteers also complete California Mandated Reporter Training (AB 506) and a Live Scan.

12. Registering as an Adult Volunteer (California / SDIC)

Troop 800 asks that at least one parent or guardian from every family register as an adult volunteer. Adult registration in SDIC combines national Scouting America requirements with California's AB 506 compliance. Plan on roughly 3–4 hours of training plus one in-person Live Scan appointment.

Step 1 — Complete Required Training

  • Safeguarding Youth Training (SYT). Log in to my.scouting.org and complete the course (about 90 minutes). Save the completion certificate.
  • California Mandated Reporter Training (AB 506). Complete the 2-hour "Volunteers" version at mandatedreporterca.com. Select "Scouting Volunteer" under the group section so the certificate is linked to SDIC.

Step 2 — Submit the Adult Application

  • Online (preferred). Visit BeAScout.org, enter ZIP code 91911, find Troop 800, and click "Apply Now."
  • Paper. Download the BSA Adult Application and Background Check Consent Form and return them to the Committee Chair.

Step 3 — Complete AB 506 Live Scan (Fingerprinting)

  • Register for the scan. Use ApplicantServices.com/SDIC. Biometrics4All offers a discounted rate of approximately $41.
  • Use the right form. You must use the council-specific Prefilled Live Scan Request form so results are routed to SDIC.
  • Find a location. Use the California DOJ Location Finder. Bring a government-issued photo ID.

Step 4 — Final Documentation and Approval

  • Upload your documents to the San Diego-Imperial Council portal.
  • Notify the troop. Let Troop 800's COR know so they can approve your application in Application Manager.

Fees

Annual adult membership with Scouting America is approximately $65, plus any local troop dues. The Live Scan fee is about $41 and is paid once. Some units reimburse the Live Scan fee.

13. Your Training Pathway — From Parent to Trained Leader

Why "Trained" matters. Every registered adult has a role and must be officially "trained" for that role. Two-deep leadership on campouts requires two trained adults — so the more trained parents we have, the more activities Troop 800 can run.

Stage 1 — Start with Committee Member Training

  • Troop Committee Challenge / Committee Member Position-Specific Training. On my.scouting.org. Self-paced, 60–90 minutes.
  • Read the Troop Committee Guidebook. A $10 paperback from the Scout Shop.
  • Attend two committee meetings before picking a specific committee job.

Stage 2 — Choose Your Next Track

Track A: Program Side (Scoutmaster or Assistant Scoutmaster)

  • Scoutmaster Position-Specific Training on my.scouting.org (~2 hours).
  • Introduction to Outdoor Leader Skills (IOLS) — in-person weekend course. Required to be considered "trained" as an SM or ASM.

Track B: Merit Badge Counselor

  • Merit Badge Counselor Orientation online, plus a separate Adult Application.
  • Get approved by the council. Once approved, Scouts can request "blue cards" to work with you.

Track C: Activity-Specific Safety Certifications

  • Hazardous Weather Training (renew every two years)
  • Safe Swim Defense — required before swimming activities
  • Safety Afloat — required before boating activities
  • Climb On Safely — required before climbing/rappelling
  • Trek Safely — required before high-adventure backpacking
  • CPR / First Aid — American Red Cross or equivalent
  • Wilderness First Aid (WFA) — 16-hour course required for Philmont

Stage 3 — Advanced Leadership Training

  • Wood Badge. Scouting America's flagship advanced leader training. Six days over two weekends plus a months-long "ticket."
  • Powder Horn. High-adventure training for leaders.
  • Trainer's EDGE. For adults who want to train other adults.
  • NAYLE. Youth training, relevant for encouraging your Scout after NYLT.

Ongoing — Stay Plugged In

  • District Roundtable (monthly)
  • University of Scouting (annual one-day conference)
  • Renew SYT every two years
  • Watch the SDIC training calendar at sdicscouting.org

15. Frequently Asked Questions

How is a troop different from a Cub Scout pack?

A Cub Scout pack is parent-led — you sit with your Cub and help them through the program. A Scouts BSA troop is youth-led — the Scouts run things, and you step back. This shift is the single biggest surprise for crossover families. Give it time; it works.

Can my Scout join mid-year?

Yes. Scouts can join at any point between ages 11 and 17. Troops are set up to bring new Scouts in on a rolling basis.

Is Troop 800 coed?

Yes — Troop 800 is a coed troop, open to boys and girls ages 11 through 17. Per BSA youth-protection policy, sleeping arrangements on campouts are always separated by gender and adults tent separately from Scouts.

What if my Scout does not want to go to a meeting or campout?

This is normal, especially in the first few months. Encourage them to go anyway — the Scouts who stick it out through the first three to six months almost always end up loving it. Talk to the Scoutmaster if reluctance persists.

How much time should I expect this to take?

Plan on three weekly meetings (~90 minutes), one PLC meeting (~90 minutes) if your Scout is in a leadership position, one weekend campout a month, and one week of summer camp a year. Older Scouts in leadership roles or working toward Eagle will invest more.

What is the Order of the Arrow?

The OA is Scouting's honor society, open to Scouts elected by their peers after meeting camping requirements. Your Scout may be eligible after their first year.

Does my Scout have to work toward Eagle?

No. Eagle is a tremendous achievement, but the journey matters more than the destination. Many Scouts have great experiences at Star or Life rank. Let your Scout set the goal.

What if we want to take a break or leave?

Talk to the Scoutmaster. Scouts can take a leave of absence; recharter is yearly, so leaving simply means not re-registering. There are no penalties, and the door stays open.
